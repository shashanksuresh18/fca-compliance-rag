## 1. Introduction and Regulatory Context

MiFID II (Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II), implemented in the EU in January 2018 and onshored into UK law through the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (as amended by the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018), requires firms to categorise clients before providing investment services. The UK equivalent is now administered by the FCA under the UK MiFID framework (following Brexit).

Client categorisation determines the level of regulatory protection afforded to each client and which obligations apply to the firm when dealing with that client.

## 2. The Three Client Categories

The FCA requires all clients to be categorised into one of three categories:

### 2.1 Retail Clients

Retail clients receive the **highest level of regulatory protection**. A client is a retail client if they do not qualify as a professional client or eligible counterparty.

Key protections for retail clients include:
- Best execution obligations (the firm must take all sufficient steps to obtain the best possible result)
- Suitability and appropriateness assessments before recommending or executing products
- Enhanced product disclosure requirements (Key Information Documents / KIDs under PRIIPs)
- Access to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) and the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS)

### 2.2 Professional Clients

Professional clients are presumed to have the expertise, experience and knowledge to make their own investment decisions and assess associated risks. They receive fewer protections than retail clients.

Professional clients are divided into:

**Per Se Professionals** (automatically qualify):
- Credit institutions and investment firms
- Insurance companies
- Large undertakings meeting two of three criteria: (i) balance sheet ≥ €20m, (ii) net turnover ≥ €40m, (iii) own funds ≥ €2m
- National and regional governments
- Central banks and supranational institutions

**Elective Professionals** (upon request):
- Retail clients may request to be reclassified as professional clients if they meet at least two of:
  1. Carried out at least 10 significant transactions per quarter over the prior 4 quarters
  2. Portfolio size exceeds €500,000
  3. Worked in financial sector in professional role requiring knowledge of relevant transactions

## 3. Eligible Counterparties

Eligible counterparties (ECPs) receive the **lowest level of regulatory protection**. They are sophisticated institutions that do not need the protections designed for retail or professional clients.

ECPs include: investment firms, credit institutions, insurance undertakings, UCITS funds and their management companies, pension funds, national governments and central banks.

An ECP classification only applies when dealing in execution and transmission of orders relating to financial instruments, or reception and transmission of orders on their own account.

## 4. Categorisation Process and Documentation

All new clients must be categorised before any investment service is provided. The categorisation process must be documented in the client onboarding file.

**Steps for Client Categorisation:**

1. **Initial Classification**: Categorise the client as retail, professional (per se or elective), or eligible counterparty based on their legal status and size criteria.
2. **Written Notification**: Inform the client in writing of their categorisation and the level of protection this affords.
3. **Re-classification Rights**: Inform clients of their right to request a different categorisation and the protections they will lose/gain.
4. **Ongoing Review**: Client classifications must be reviewed annually or when material changes in client circumstances occur.

## 5. Reclassification ("Opting Up" and "Opting Down")

### 5.1 Opting Down (Client Protection Increase)
Professional clients and ECPs may opt down to a higher-protection category. A professional client can request retail protection. The firm must provide this if requested and document the change.

### 5.2 Opting Up (Client Protection Decrease)
Retail clients may request professional status if they meet the elective professional criteria (Section 2.2). The firm must:
- Conduct an adequate assessment of the client's expertise and knowledge
- Warn the client in writing of the protections they will lose
- Obtain clear written confirmation from the client that they are aware of the consequences
- Document all steps in the client file

Firms may not solicit clients to opt up — the request must come from the client.
